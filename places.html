Places I Recommend

Strawberry Falls

While perhaps not as exciting as the other entries on this list, Strawberry Falls perfectly encapsulates the Pacific Northwest's unique ecology as the short trek through the inumberable evergreens spills way into the sprawling sandy beach, its horizon dotted by towering seastacks that are jaw dropping no matter the time of day or weather.

Ideal for day hikes or overnight stays, Strawberry falls has the best campsites on this list as many are situated right on the beach ensuring an unforgettable sunrise. Also, because it's not located in the Puget Sound and instead facing the Pacific Ocean it's great for surfing.

Fort Worden

Constructed between the late nineteenth and early twentieth century in response to mounting concerns over America's lackluster coastal defenses, these so called Endicott Period forts were once state of the art as they employed breachloading cannons and reinforced concrete defenses. The Second World War would show that these concerns weren't entirely unfounded, making it all the more fortunant that these coastal batteries were never used. Now Fort Worden along with its many siblings including Fort Casey, and Flagler stand in silent vigil overlooking the Salish Sea.

While by no means difficult, this turn of the century military installation will take several hours to explore and many of the bunkers themselves are unlit. Also, overnight camping within the bunkers themselves is not permitted. I'd highly recommend Fort Worden for first timers.

Tubal Cain Mine

As the name suggests this trail features an abandoned copper mine. Closed sometime in the early twenties following nonexistant profits the shaft itself is completely flooded and for those who value self preservation the only evidence of its existence being the mine entrace itself and the abandoend equipment that lay scattered throughout the campgrounds in the form of perforated boilers and rusted minecarts. I've heard some people venture past the entrance, however swimming in freezing, stagnant water with no light miles away from emergency responders seems ill-advised.

But if that wasn't intriguing enough climb a few miles more any you'll find the mangled remains of a B-17 bomber. Retrofitted for non-combat use after WW2 the tail of the aircraft clipped the top of the Cascades sending the plane hurdling into the value below. From the wreckage its a wonder anyone survived as its only recognizable by its landing gear and a few propellerless engines.

Definitely the hardest hike of the three. The initial trek to the mine takes several hours and features a precarious washout next to a cliff. After that there's getting to the bomber and when I said climb I meant it. The incline feels a few degrees short of rock climbing and that isn't helped by the several streams crosscutting the trail up lined with slippery rocks. A little strenuous for a day hike, the plane itself crash landed in a marsh so I wouldn't recommend bringing a new pair of shoes if you want to get to each major piece of debris. Also mosquitos, lots of mosquitos.
